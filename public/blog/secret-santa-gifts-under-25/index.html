Secret Santa gifts under £25

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

A good Secret Santa gift is useful, slightly nicer than the person would buy themselves, and safe to open in front of colleagues. Aim for good consumables, quality desk objects, or something tied to one thing you know about them. Respect the cap exactly — going over isn’t generous, it’s awkward for everyone.

Office Secret Santa is a genuinely distinct gifting problem. You may have never spoken to the person. There’s a hard budget cap. It gets opened in a room, in front of everyone, possibly with commentary. And whatever you choose says something about you to the whole team.

Most people respond by buying a joke, because a joke feels safe in a room. It usually isn’t — it’s just the failure that happens fastest. Here’s a better approach.

What you’re actually optimising for

A Secret Santa gift has to clear three bars at once, and most bad ones fail at least two.

  • It has to survive being opened in public. No private jokes, nothing that requires explanation, nothing that could embarrass anyone.
  • It has to be useful enough to keep. The bar is low, but “would a normal adult take this home deliberately?” eliminates most novelty items.
  • It has to fit the cap. Exactly, not approximately, and definitely not over.

Anything that clears all three is a success. You are not trying to be the standout; you are trying to give somebody something they’re quietly pleased with. That’s a much easier and more achievable target.

The rule that decides most cases

Buy something the person uses regularly, one clear step nicer than the version they’d buy for themselves. That single instruction handles the vast majority of Secret Santas without you needing to know anything personal at all.

Categories that consistently work

Excellent consumables

The most reliable option, for the same reason it works everywhere else: nothing to store, nothing to display, quality immediately obvious. Coffee from a real roaster with a roast date on it. Proper loose-leaf tea. Single-origin chocolate. A small bottle of very good olive oil. Interesting hot sauce if you know they like heat.

The upgrade from supermarket to specialist is enormous in perceived value and costs almost nothing. A £12 bag of coffee from a good roaster reads as far more thoughtful than a £20 boxed gift set, because one was chosen and the other was picked up.

Desk objects of genuine quality

People spend hours a day at a desk surrounded by things they never chose. Improving one of them is quietly appreciated every working day.

  • A properly good pen — a real one, not a branded freebie
  • A well-bound notebook, plain, no slogan
  • A small plant that survives neglect
  • A decent mug — heavy, plain, not novelty
  • A cable tidy, a laptop stand, a monitor riser
  • Good hand cream, particularly in winter

Something built on one known fact

If you know a single thing — they cycle in, they’re always cold, they drink four coffees a day, they’ve got a dog — build the gift on it. Specificity is what separates a gift from a transaction, and it takes no extra money at all.

What to avoid

  • Anything about their body or appearance. No exceptions, no matter how kindly meant.
  • Anything about their work performance, including the “world’s okayest employee” genre.
  • Alcohol, unless you know for certain they drink.
  • Anything requiring an explanation to make sense in a room.
  • Extremely personal items — fragrance, anything worn close to the skin.
  • Prank gifts. The person has to sit in a room and be gracious about it.
  • Anything bulky. They may be on a train.

Ask yourself: if the most senior person in the building watched this being unwrapped, would it be fine? If there’s any hesitation, pick again.

When you draw someone you don’t know at all

The most common Secret Santa scenario in a larger office, and the one people panic about. The solution is thirty seconds of research rather than a bigger budget.

Ask whoever sits nearest them one low-key question: what do they drink, or what’s always on their desk? Nobody will connect it to Secret Santa, and the answer converts immediately into a gift. Failing that, look at their desk yourself — a plant that’s struggling, a mug from a place, a photo, a keyboard buried under paper. Every one of those is a lead.

If you genuinely can’t find anything, buy the best consumable in your budget and stop worrying. A bag of coffee from a good roaster is a correct answer, not a surrender. The person who receives it will drink it and think well of whoever chose it, which is the entire objective.

The variants: Bad Santa, White Elephant and themed swaps

Some offices run formats where the rules are different, and applying standard Secret Santa thinking to them misfires.

In a steal-based swap (often called White Elephant or Yankee Swap), gifts get taken from each other, which means the goal shifts: you want something broadly desirable rather than personally tailored, since it may end up with anyone. Good consumables, a decent bottle if drinking is fine in your workplace, or a genuinely nice desk object all work well here.

In a deliberately silly format, the joke is sanctioned and expectations are different — but the safety rules still apply. Nothing about a person’s body, work or private life, and nothing you’d be uncomfortable having read aloud. Aim the humour at a situation everyone shares rather than at an individual.

In a themed swap — books, plants, something homemade — the theme is doing you a favour. It narrows the field enormously, which is exactly the constraint that makes choosing easy. Lean into it rather than looking for a loophole.

If you’re the one organising it

A few decisions made upfront prevent almost all of the friction. Set the cap clearly and state that it’s a limit rather than a target. Make participation genuinely optional and say so, since not everyone celebrates and not everyone has spare money in December.

Collect any allergies or dietary restrictions when people sign up — it takes one extra line on the form and prevents the most common bad outcome. And set a clear time and place for the exchange, because the worst version of Secret Santa is the one where half the gifts sit in a cupboard until January.

The budget cap is a rule, not a suggestion

Every Secret Santa has one person who spends triple the limit, and it is never as generous as it feels. It makes everyone else’s gift look mean, embarrasses whoever drew that person, and raises the informal expectation for next year.

If the cap is £25, land between £18 and £25. If you want to be generous with a colleague you like, do it separately and privately — not inside a structure everybody else is following.

The related trap is spending the full cap on one large mediocre item rather than most of it on one small excellent one. Quality reads instantly; size doesn’t.

Presentation is disproportionately powerful here

Because everything is opened in the same room, presentation is directly comparable in a way it never is with private gifts. Wrapping properly takes four minutes and visibly separates your gift from the ones handed over in a carrier bag.

Add a small card with one specific sentence — something about working with them, or about the year. Anonymous formats make this harder, but even “I hope this makes the 8am meetings slightly better” converts a bag of coffee into something the person remembers.

If your team is remote

Remote Secret Santa has one extra constraint: it has to post well and arrive on time. Favour things that are flat, unbreakable and light — good coffee, chocolate, a notebook, a gift card with something small attached. Order earlier than feels necessary, and if the exchange happens on a video call, wrap it properly, because packaging is the only presentation anyone will see.

If you get something you don’t like

Worth a line, because it happens to everyone. React warmly in the room regardless — somebody made an effort with very little information, which is difficult, and the exchange is a social ritual rather than a test of accuracy.

Afterwards, do whatever you like with it. Passing something on to someone who’ll use it is not ungrateful; leaving it in a drawer for two years is the genuinely wasteful outcome. Nobody who gave a Secret Santa gift expects it to be kept forever.

The short version

Buy something ordinary but genuinely good. Respect the cap. Skip the joke. Wrap it properly. Then, if you know one true thing about the person, let that pick the category.

That’s the entire method, and it produces the outcome you actually want: not the biggest laugh in the room, but a thing that quietly ends up on their desk in March.

Frequently asked questions

What's a good Secret Santa gift for a coworker I don't know?

Excellent coffee or tea, good chocolate, a genuinely nice notebook, a proper desk plant, or quality hand cream. All are useful, none require knowing their taste, and all read as considered rather than random.

Should I go funny or useful?

Useful, with at most a light touch of humour. Joke gifts get a laugh in the room and then live in a drawer. The person who gives something people actually keep is remembered far more warmly than the person who got the biggest laugh.

Can I go over the budget?

Don’t. Exceeding the cap makes everyone else’s gift look inadequate and quietly pressures next year’s limit upwards. If the cap feels low, the answer is one very good small thing rather than more spend.

What should I never buy for a work Secret Santa?

Anything about their appearance, weight, age, relationship status or work performance. Alcohol unless you know they drink. Anything with a joke that would be awkward if read aloud by a manager. And nothing so large they have to carry it home on public transport.

What if I get someone I've never spoken to?

Ask someone who sits near them one question: what do they drink, or what’s on their desk? Thirty seconds of research turns a generic gift into a specific one. Failing that, good consumables are always a correct answer.
