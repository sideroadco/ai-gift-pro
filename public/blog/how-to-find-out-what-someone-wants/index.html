How to find out what someone wants (without asking)

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

Direct questions fail because most people can’t recall wants on demand. What works instead is asking about irritations, routines and plans rather than desires — “what’s been annoying you?” produces usable answers where “what do you want?” produces a shrug. Keep a running note year-round and the problem disappears.

The standard advice for gift-giving is to think about what the person wants. The standard problem is that nobody knows what anybody wants, including the person themselves. Ask someone directly and you get the familiar non-answer: oh, I don’t know, anything, honestly don’t worry about it.

This is usually treated as politeness or evasion. It’s neither. It’s a straightforward feature of how human memory works, and once you understand the mechanism, you can route around it entirely.

Why direct questions fail

Wants are contextual. You discover that you need a better tin opener while wrestling with a tin, not while sitting on a sofa being asked what you’d like for your birthday. The desire is generated by the situation, and it evaporates the moment the situation ends.

So when you ask someone in the abstract, you’re asking them to run a search with no cue. Most people genuinely come up blank, and the ones who don’t tend to name the most recent thing they saw advertised, which is rarely what they most want.

There’s a second factor too. Naming a want out loud feels like making a claim on someone else’s money, which many people find uncomfortable. The instinct is to deflect, hedge, or downgrade the request — which is why the answers you do get are so often smaller than what the person would actually love.

The reframe

Stop trying to extract a want. Go looking for friction instead. Friction is specific, memorable and easy to talk about, and every point of friction in someone’s day is a gift waiting to be bought.

Questions that actually produce answers

These work because they’re not recognisably about presents. Ask them in normal conversation, ideally well before the occasion, and pay attention to the answers rather than steering them.

  • “What’s been annoying you lately?” The single best question. Produces broken things, worn-out things, and daily irritations.
  • “What would you do with a completely free Saturday?” Reveals what they wish they had more of — and points at experiences and hobbies.
  • “What have you been meaning to get round to?” Surfaces the stalled projects. Half of them need equipment; the other half need help.
  • “What’s the best thing you’ve bought this year?” Tells you what they value and how they judge quality. Follow-up gifts practically write themselves.
  • “Is there anything you keep almost buying and then not buying?” This one is almost unfairly effective. Everyone has an item stuck in that queue.
  • “What would you eat if you didn’t have to be sensible?” Converts instantly into a consumable gift.

The common thread is that none of them require the person to perform a want. They’re invitations to complain, to daydream, or to talk about something they’re already thinking about — all of which people do easily.

What to notice when you’re not asking anything

Most of the information you need is already in front of you and simply isn’t being recorded. The gap between people who are “good at presents” and people who aren’t is almost never taste — it’s that the first group notices things and the second group doesn’t write them down.

Look at what’s worn out. The wallet held together by habit. The trainers with no tread. The chopping board with a crack in it. People tolerate deteriorating objects far longer than they’d admit, because replacing them is never urgent enough to reach the top of the list. That’s exactly what a gift is for.

Look at what’s makeshift. A book propping up a laptop. A mug of pens. Headphones with tape on the cable. Every improvisation is a problem someone has solved badly and would love to have solved properly.

Look at what they do repeatedly. The daily routine is the richest seam available, because anything you improve there gets appreciated every single day rather than once. Coffee in the morning, a walk with the dog, reading before bed, the commute.

Listen for the word “someday”. “I’d love to learn to …” and “one day I’ll get round to …” are direct statements of desire that people don’t recognise as such. They’re also the easiest gifts to give, because the person has already done the wanting for you.

The habit that makes this trivial: keep a note

Everything above is useless if you rely on remembering it in December. The single highest-return change you can make to your gift-giving is a note on your phone with a heading for each person you buy for.

When someone mentions something, add a line. When something breaks, add a line. It takes eight seconds and feels faintly ridiculous the first few times. Then the next occasion arrives, you open the note, and instead of panic you have a list of things the person told you they wanted, in their own words, months ago.

What you overhearWhat goes in the note
“This kettle takes forever”Kettle — wants faster/temperature control
“I’ve always wanted to try pottery”Pottery class — look for local beginner course
“My back kills me at that desk”Desk setup — chair cushion, laptop stand
“I never have a decent pen”Good pen — easy stocking filler
“We keep meaning to go to X”Book it. Date, not voucher.

Using other people properly

Asking someone close to them is standard advice, and it usually fails because people ask the wrong question. “What should I get her?” produces the same blank as asking her directly, because you’ve outsourced the hard part rather than the easy part.

Ask for observations instead of recommendations. What have they been talking about? What did they do last weekend? What’s broken in the house that nobody gets round to fixing? What did they buy recently that they’re pleased with? Anyone who lives with the person can answer all of those instantly, and every answer is convertible.

There’s also a coordination benefit. Telling one other person what you’re thinking of prevents the classic disaster where three people give versions of the same thing, and it occasionally surfaces the “oh, don’t — she’s already bought one” that saves you.

Wishlists, and how to use them well

If someone keeps a public wishlist, use it. The idea that this is cheating has cost a lot of people a lot of good gifts. Somebody wrote down what they want and made it visible; ignoring that on principle is a strange kind of pride.

The refinement is to treat it as a map of taste rather than a shopping list. If they’ve listed three books by one author, the fourth book is fine but a signed edition or the author’s lesser-known first novel is better. If they’ve listed a mid-range version of something, the better version is a genuine upgrade you know they’ll use.

Buying the exact item is never wrong. Buying the thing next to it is what makes the gift feel authored rather than fulfilled.

Approaches that don’t work

  • The leading question. “So do you like jewellery?” asked in November fools nobody and spoils the surprise.
  • Interrogation. Three questions in a row about hobbies is conspicuous. Spread them across weeks.
  • Taking “nothing” literally on the first pass. Usually the start of a conversation, not the end of one.
  • Trusting a single offhand comment from years ago. People’s interests move. Confirm before you spend heavily on an old data point.
  • Assuming a hobby means they want more hobby equipment. Enthusiasts often have very specific preferences and already own the obvious things. For a serious hobbyist, consumables they get through, or a lesson, beat guessing at kit.

The people who seem to have a magical instinct for presents are not more intuitive than you. They are simply writing things down.

The special case: buying for a long-term partner

Everything above gets harder with the person you live with, which seems backwards until you think about why. You have more information than anyone, but you also have the least novelty available: if they wanted it and it was affordable, one of you probably bought it already.

The useful move here is to stop looking for gaps in their possessions and start looking for things they’ve decided they can’t justify. Long-term partners are usually running a shared budget in their heads, and there’s a category of purchase — slightly indulgent, entirely for them, not strictly necessary — that quietly never gets approved. That category is where the good gifts live.

The second move is to give time rather than objects, and to give it in a way that requires no organising from them. Taking the children for a day, handling the thing they dread, booking the weekend and packing the bags. For someone carrying a shared mental load, the removal of that load is worth more than anything you could wrap.

When you genuinely have nothing

Sometimes there’s no time, no access and no information — a new colleague, a distant relative, a plus-one. In that case, stop trying to be perceptive and fall back on the categories that don’t require knowledge: an excellent consumable, a small tool of unusually good quality, or an experience you arrange and handle.

These are not consolation prizes. They’re the correct answer to a genuine information gap, and they land far better than a strained attempt at personalisation built on a guess. Trying to appear to know someone you don’t is more obvious, and more awkward, than simply giving them something very good.

And then start the note. Next year, for that person, you’ll have something to work with — which is the entire trick, and the only one that consistently works.

Frequently asked questions

Why can't people answer “what do you want?”

Because it asks them to search their memory with no cue. Wanting is usually triggered by context — you notice you need something while doing the thing. Sitting in a living room in December, that context is absent, so the search returns nothing. It isn’t evasiveness, it’s how recall works.

What's the single best question to ask?

“What’s been annoying you lately?” People who can’t name a want can always name a frustration, and frustrations convert directly into gifts: a dying kettle, a bag that hurts, a pan everything sticks to. Ask it casually, weeks before the occasion, and just listen.

Is looking at someone's wishlist cheating?

No, and treating it as cheating is why people ignore a perfectly good source. A wishlist is an explicit invitation. The refinement is to use it as evidence of taste rather than a shopping list — buy the thing adjacent to what’s on it, or the better version, so it still feels chosen rather than collected.

How do I find out without ruining the surprise?

Ask about the category, never the item. “Do you still use that camera much?” tells you whether an accessory would land, without revealing anything. Questions about routines and hobbies are invisible; questions about specific products are not.

What if I ask their friends or partner?

Do it, but ask a better question than they expect. “What should I get them?” gets a shrug from anyone. “What have they been talking about lately?” or “what’s broken in the house that never gets fixed?” gets you something you can actually buy.
