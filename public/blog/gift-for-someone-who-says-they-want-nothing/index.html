What to get someone who says they don’t want anything

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

When someone says they want nothing, they usually mean one of four things: they don’t want you to spend money, they hate clutter, they genuinely can’t think of anything, or they don’t want the pressure of being given something. Match the gift to the reason, and buy something consumable, replacing, experiential, or maintenance-related rather than a new object.

“Don’t get me anything.” It’s the least helpful sentence in gift-giving, and almost everyone says it at some point. The problem isn’t that it’s a lie — it usually isn’t. The problem is that it’s an answer to a different question than the one you asked.

You asked what they want. They answered whether they need anything. Those are not the same, and treating them as the same is how you end up in a shop on the last afternoon before a birthday, holding a candle you don’t believe in.

What people actually mean when they say it

Before you can buy anything, you have to work out which version of “nothing” you’re dealing with. In practice there are four, and they call for completely different responses.

What they sayWhat’s usually underneathWhat works
“Don’t spend money on me”Guilt about cost, or a sense that money is tight for youSomething inexpensive and clearly thoughtful, or something you made
“I don’t need more stuff”Genuine clutter fatigue — often someone in a small home or mid-declutterConsumables, experiences, or a replacement for something they own
“I honestly can’t think of anything”They’re not a wants-driven person and find the question stressfulSolve a small friction in their daily routine
“Really, nothing, I mean it”Dislikes being the centre of attention or feeling indebtedSomething small, given casually, with no ceremony

Notice that only one of those four is about money. The most common one in my experience is the second — the person isn’t being humble, they’re being practical. They’ve looked at the shelf in their hallway and concluded that another decorative object is a burden, not a treat.

The test that sorts it out

Ask a different question and listen to the shape of the answer. Not “what do you want?” but “what’s been annoying you lately?” People who can’t name a want can almost always name an irritation. A dead kettle, a bag strap that digs in, a pan everything sticks to. That’s your gift, and it arrives disguised as small talk.

Five categories that work on people who want nothing

The unifying principle: don’t add an object to their life. Every category below either gets used up, replaces something, or takes up no space at all.

1. Consumables they’d never buy at that quality

The single most reliable category. Good coffee, real olive oil, proper chocolate, an interesting hot sauce, decent tea. The logic is simple: they will finish it, so it can never become clutter, and the version you buy is nicer than the version they buy for themselves, so it registers as a treat rather than a grocery.

The mistake people make here is buying a hamper of twelve things. One excellent item beats a basket of adequate ones. A single bar of genuinely good chocolate says “I know what you like.” A gift basket says “I bought a gift basket.”

2. The upgrade to something they already use daily

This is the highest-conversion category in the entire exercise, and it’s under-used because it feels boring to the giver. It isn’t boring to the receiver. Look at what they touch every morning and buy the better version.

  • They make coffee every day → a proper burr grinder or a temperature-control kettle
  • They read in bed → a decent clip-on light instead of the phone torch
  • They cook constantly → one really good chef’s knife, or a whetstone for the one they have
  • They walk the dog at 6am → a genuinely warm hat, not a novelty one

Because it replaces something rather than adding to it, the “no more stuff” objection disappears entirely. You have made their pile of possessions better without making it bigger.

3. Maintenance and repair for things they love

Deeply underrated. If someone owns a good leather bag, a cast-iron pan, a bike, a record player, or a pair of boots they’ve had for a decade, the thing that keeps that object alive is a gift that respects what they already chose. Leather conditioner, a chain cleaning kit, a stylus brush, a re-sole voucher from a local cobbler.

The message this sends is unusually good: I noticed what you care about, and I want it to last. That’s a hard sentiment to buy directly, and this category buys it for about fifteen pounds.

4. Experiences with a date attached

Experiences dodge the clutter problem completely, but they have a failure mode: the voucher that sits in a drawer until it expires, which quietly becomes a chore you gave someone. The fix is to attach a date and do the admin yourself.

“A cooking class sometime” is a burden. “We’re doing a pasta class on the 14th, it’s booked, I’ve told your partner, wear something you don’t mind getting flour on” is a gift. The work you removed is the present.

5. The thing they’ve mentioned twice and never bought

Everybody has one. It’s usually mid-priced, mildly indulgent, and stuck in a permanent queue behind more responsible purchases. A good umbrella. A weighted blanket. A proper set of headphones. Noise-cancelling anything.

These items don’t get bought because they’re never urgent. That’s precisely what makes them a good gift — you’re not guessing at a want, you’re resolving a stalemate they’ve been having with themselves.

What to avoid, specifically

The reason someone said “nothing” matters most here, because the wrong gift actively confirms their fear that gift-giving is a hassle.

  • Decorative objects. Signs with words on them, ornaments, anything whose only function is to occupy a surface. This is the exact thing the clutter-averse person was warning you about.
  • Anything requiring assembly or a learning curve they didn’t ask for. A gift that comes with homework is a task.
  • Clothing in a size you’re guessing at. Now they have to manage a return.
  • Big-ticket surprises. The person who said “don’t spend money” and receives something expensive feels the debt, not the delight.
  • Gift cards to shops they don’t use. This is homework with a deadline.

The honest test: in six months, will this be in use, used up, or in a cupboard? If the answer is cupboard, it’s not a gift, it’s a transfer of storage responsibility.

How to hand it over

This part gets ignored and it matters more than the object. Someone who asked for nothing is often uncomfortable with the ritual, not the item. So strip the ritual out.

Give it early or give it casually. Skip the speech about how long you searched — that converts a present into an obligation, because now they have to perform gratitude proportional to your effort. “This made me think of you” is enough. Say it, hand it over, change the subject.

And if they protest anyway, a useful line: “It’s not a big thing, I just wanted you to have it.” That closes the loop without making them negotiate.

A worked example

Say it’s your mother-in-law. She has said, firmly, that she doesn’t need anything, she’s trying to have less in the house, and please don’t make a fuss.

Under the old approach you’d buy a candle, and she’d put it in the drawer with the other candles. Under this framework you’d notice that she makes a pot of tea every afternoon and the teapot has a chipped lid, and that she mentioned in passing that the garden centre stopped stocking the seed she likes.

So: a good replacement teapot, or two packets of that seed with a note. Small, unfussy, consumable or replacing. Nothing about it violates a single thing she asked for — and it demonstrates you were listening, which is the actual content of any gift.

If you want the shortcut, sorted by who they are

The framework above works for anyone. But the same person saying “nothing” means slightly different things depending on the relationship, and it’s worth being blunt about it.

  • A parent. Usually means “don’t spend your money on me.” They almost always want time or something you made or organised. Booking the thing, doing the admin, or turning up with lunch outperforms any object.
  • A partner. Rarely literal. Often a test of whether you’ve been paying attention all year. Go for the thing they mentioned once in April and assumed you’d forgotten.
  • A friend. Usually about not wanting to trigger an arms race of reciprocal spending. Keep it small and funny and specific to an in-joke; the point is the recognition, not the item.
  • A colleague. Genuinely might mean nothing. Consumable and shared is the safe zone — good coffee for the team, not a personal object.
  • Someone recently bereaved or unwell. Often means they have no capacity for admin. Practical help, delivered food, or a standing offer with a specific date beats anything wrapped.

When you should genuinely buy nothing

There is a version of this where the right answer really is to respect it completely, and it deserves saying plainly because most articles skip it.

If someone has set an explicit, ongoing boundary — “we’re not doing presents between adults any more” — that isn’t modesty, and overriding it isn’t generous. It puts them in the position of having to reciprocate next time or feel rude, which is exactly what they were trying to avoid.

The same applies to someone in financial difficulty who has quietly opted out because they can’t match it. A gift there can land as a reminder of the gap. Time, help, or a shared meal where you happen to pay is kinder than a wrapped object.

The distinction is simple: a one-off “oh don’t worry about me” is deflection. A standing arrangement is a boundary. Deflection you can gently work around. Boundaries you honour.

That’s the whole method. Stop asking what they want. Work out what they’d use, what they’d run out of, or what they’d replace, and buy that instead.

Frequently asked questions

Does “I don't want anything” ever actually mean nothing?

Rarely, and it’s usually obvious when it does. If someone has explicitly asked you to stop exchanging gifts as a standing arrangement between adults, take that at face value — that’s a boundary, not modesty. What’s different is the person who says it once, in response to a single occasion, while still giving gifts themselves. That’s deflection, not a policy.

Is it rude to buy something anyway?

Not if you respect the reason behind the refusal. Buying a large, expensive, attention-drawing object for someone who asked for nothing can feel like you overrode them. Buying something small, consumable, or genuinely useful reads as attentive. The failure mode isn’t spending money — it’s creating an obligation they now have to reciprocate or store.

What if they get annoyed?

Keep the receipt and keep it low-key. Hand it over without ceremony and without a speech about how much thought you put in — that’s what creates pressure. “I saw this and thought of you, no big deal” lets them off the hook emotionally. If it’s consumable, the problem solves itself within a month.

How much should I spend on someone who said not to?

Less than you normally would, deliberately. A person who said “nothing” and receives something expensive often feels indebted rather than delighted. Undershooting your usual amount by a third is a good rule — it signals you listened while still marking the occasion.

What's the single safest option?

An upgrade to something they already buy for themselves. Not a new category, not a new hobby — the nicer version of a thing already in their life. It can’t become clutter because it replaces something, and it can’t be wrong because they’ve already told you they use it.
