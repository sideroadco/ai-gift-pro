Gifts for someone who has everything

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

Someone who “has everything” doesn’t need more things — they need something they wouldn’t have chosen for themselves. The four categories that work: the best version of a boring necessity, a consumable at a quality they’d never justify, something with provenance or a story, and access rather than objects. Novelty gadgets are the single worst answer.

“They have everything.” It’s never literally true, and stating it that way is what makes the problem unsolvable. Nobody has everything. What this person actually has is enough disposable income and enough decisiveness that any gap in their life gets closed immediately, by them, to their own taste.

That reframing matters, because it tells you exactly where the remaining openings are. If they buy anything they want as soon as they want it, then the only things missing from their life are the things they wouldn’t buy: too indulgent to justify, too obscure to find, or too much admin to arrange.

Why the usual approach fails

The instinct with a hard-to-buy-for person is to go weird. If they own every normal thing, the logic goes, buy an abnormal thing. This is how people end up giving a whisky-flavoured toothpaste, a novelty desk toy, or a machine that makes a single perfect ice sphere.

It fails for a reason worth understanding. A person with a curated life has already exercised taste over every object around them. A novelty item is, by definition, something nobody chose on merit — it exists to be surprising. Dropping one into a considered home doesn’t read as playful, it reads as a mismatch. And they can tell you bought it because you were stuck.

The core insight

For most people, a gift closes a gap between what they have and what they’d like. For this person, no such gap exists. So the gift has to supply something money doesn’t: your attention, your specific taste, a story, or access. Those are the only four currencies left.

1. The best version of something boring

People who buy freely still under-invest in unglamorous categories. Nobody wakes up excited to upgrade their tea towels. This is a reliable blind spot and it’s where the best gifts for this person live.

  • Household basics at a quality they’d never shop for: heavyweight linen tea towels, a genuinely good doormat, proper wooden hangers, dense cotton bath sheets.
  • The tool they own a bad version of: a real corkscrew instead of the free one, sharp kitchen scissors, a heavy pepper mill that actually grinds.
  • Maintenance for things they already own: a whetstone for their knives, leather balm for their bag, a proper garment brush for good coats.

The pleasure here is specific and slightly funny: it’s the delight of discovering the version of a mundane object that’s three times better than the one you’ve tolerated for a decade. Someone who appreciates quality will appreciate it exactly because they’d never have spent the time sourcing it themselves.

2. Consumables at an unjustifiable quality

The most dependable category for this recipient, for a mechanical reason: consumables cannot duplicate anything they own and cannot become clutter. There is no risk of the gift being redundant, because it disappears.

The rule: one excellent thing, not a basket of adequate ones. A hamper is a hedge. A single item you can talk about for thirty seconds — where it’s from, why you picked it — is a gift with a point of view, which is the thing they can’t buy.

3. Objects with provenance

Anything that carries a story can’t be replicated by their own credit card, because the story is the product. This is where money genuinely stops being the deciding factor.

  • Something old. A first edition of a book they love, a vintage map of a place that matters to them, an out-of-print record.
  • Something local to a shared memory. Wine from the region you travelled together, a print of a place from their childhood.
  • Something made by a person you can name. A potter’s mug, a small piece from a maker whose work you can point to.
  • Something archival. A newspaper from the day they were born, framed properly rather than in the novelty packaging.

The reason this works: the person who has everything has plenty of nice things and very few irreplaceable ones. Provenance is a category their own shopping habits systematically miss, because you can’t search for it — you have to stumble on it or know them well.

4. Access, not objects

The most valuable thing to a person with money is almost always time, or entry to something they can’t simply purchase.

  • The hard-to-get reservation, booked, with a date, with the babysitter already arranged if that applies.
  • A membership to something that will keep giving all year — a gallery, a botanic garden, a lecture series.
  • A lesson from someone excellent in a thing they already love. Not a beginner class in a random hobby — an hour with a real expert in the thing they’re already into.
  • Removed admin. Paying for the thing they keep putting off: the deep clean, the photo albums finally printed, the box of slides digitised.

The most quietly luxurious gift you can give a person who can buy anything is a task they’ve been avoiding, completed, with no further action required from them.

What to skip entirely

  • Novelty and gag gifts. Covered above. The laugh lasts eleven seconds.
  • “Luxury” versions of things they didn’t want. An expensive object in a category they don’t care about is just a more awkward wrong gift.
  • Anything with their initials on it unless you are certain. Monogramming removes the ability to return, re-gift, or quietly ignore.
  • Tech they’d have already bought. If they wanted the new gadget, they own the new gadget. You are buying a duplicate.
  • Gift sets assembled by a shop. Visible outsourcing of the thinking.

How to actually decide, in ten minutes

Run three questions in order and stop at the first one that produces an answer.

  1. What have they mentioned wanting to do, rather than own? Learn, visit, taste, see. That points to access.
  2. What do they use every single day that is slightly worse than it could be? That points to the upgrade.
  3. What do they consume with obvious pleasure? That points to the exceptional consumable.

If all three come up blank, you don’t have a gift problem — you have an information problem, and the honest move is to ask someone closer to them one specific question: “What’s she been into lately?” That is not cheating. It’s research.

What to buy at each budget

Budget matters less here than almost anywhere else in gifting, but people still want a starting point, so here is an honest one.

Under £20. This is consumable territory and it is not a compromise. One outstanding bar of chocolate, a tin of good oil, a bag of coffee from a roaster they’ve never tried, a jar of raw local honey. At this price your job is to be specific rather than generous, and specificity is free.

£20–60. The upgrade zone. This is where the boring-but-excellent household object lives: the heavy pepper mill, the linen tea towels, the whetstone, the proper umbrella. Also the sweet spot for a secondhand book with a real history, or a print that means something.

£60–150. Access starts to open up. A year’s membership somewhere they’ll actually go. A lesson with someone genuinely good. A dinner booked and paid for at the place they mentioned. At this level, spend on removing friction rather than on adding another object.

Above that. Be careful. Large sums given to someone who can spend larger sums tend to feel like an attempt to keep up, and it can quietly embarrass both of you. If you’re spending this much, spend it on something with provenance or on an experience you share, not on a product they could have ordered on a Tuesday afternoon.

How to find the information without ruining the surprise

Most of the failure in this category is an information failure, not a taste failure. You’re guessing because you don’t have data, and asking directly gets you “oh, nothing.”

Better questions produce better answers, and they work because they’re not recognisably about presents. Try asking what they’ve been reading or watching lately, what they’d do with a completely free Saturday, what they’ve been meaning to get round to, or what they’d eat if calories didn’t count. Every one of those produces an actionable answer from a person who cannot name a single thing they want.

If you have access to someone who lives with them, one question beats all of the above: “What’s broken or annoying in the house that never gets fixed?” The answer is frequently a gift, and it is always a welcome one.

The part people miss

Someone who has everything is, in gift terms, someone for whom the object has stopped carrying meaning. Everything on their shelves arrived without friction. So the meaning has to come from somewhere else — from evidence that you paid attention, from a story the object carries, or from effort you spent that they didn’t have to.

Which is why the twelve-pound answer routinely beats the two-hundred-pound one. You are not trying to fill a gap in their possessions. You’re trying to give them the one thing their own money has never once bought them: proof that somebody was paying close attention.

Frequently asked questions

Why do novelty gifts fail so badly with this person?

Because novelty is a substitute for knowing someone. A quirky gadget signals “I couldn’t think of anything so I bought something surprising,” and a person with a well-furnished life reads that instantly. The gift gets a laugh, then a shelf, then a charity shop. Specific beats surprising every time.

Isn't a gift card the honest answer?

It’s honest but it’s not a gift — it’s a transfer with a logo on it. For someone who can already buy what they want, money adds nothing. The whole value of a present to this person is the part they couldn’t have generated themselves: your attention, your taste, or access they didn’t have.

What about experiences?

Strong, with one condition: you have to do the organising. A wealthy, busy person doesn’t lack the ability to book a restaurant — they lack the time and the prompt. The gift is the reservation, the date in the diary, and the logistics handled, not the voucher.

How do I compete when they can afford better than I can?

Stop competing on price, because you’ll lose and the attempt is visible. Compete on specificity. A twelve-pound thing that proves you listened outperforms a two-hundred-pound thing that anyone could have chosen. Their whole problem is that money has stopped generating novelty for them.

What's the safest single option?

An exceptional version of something they use up. Great olive oil, single-origin coffee, proper salt, a serious bottle of something. It can’t clutter, it can’t duplicate, it’s consumed with pleasure, and quality is immediately obvious to someone with taste.
