Gifts for readers (that aren’t books)

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

Buying a specific book for a committed reader is risky: they may have read it, own it, or have deliberately avoided it. Safer and often better are the things that support reading — light, comfort, storage, and access. If you do buy a book, buy a beautiful edition of something they already love rather than something new.

It seems obvious to buy a reader a book. It’s also the option most likely to miss, for a reason that only becomes clear once you think about how committed readers actually operate.

They have a list. It’s long. They’ve read the well-reviewed things, dismissed some deliberately, and have particular feelings about genres you may not know. A book chosen from outside that context is a coin flip — and worse, an unread gift book sits on the shelf as a quiet obligation.

Buy the infrastructure, not the content

The reliable move is to improve the experience of reading rather than to supply the material. Everyone who reads a lot has friction in how they do it, and almost nobody spends money fixing it.

Light

The most under-bought item in this entire category. Most people read in bad light — a ceiling bulb, a phone torch, a lamp aimed at the wrong angle. A good clip-on light with warm tone and adjustable brightness costs very little and gets used every single night.

Comfort

A reading pillow with arms for people who read sitting up in bed. A decent throw. A footstool. A lap desk. Reading is a physical activity done in one position for hours, and the difference between comfortable and slightly-wrong is enormous over a whole evening.

Storage and organisation

Anyone who reads a lot eventually has a storage problem. Good bookends, a small shelf for the to-be-read pile, a proper bookstand for cookbooks or reference, or a nice box for the ones being passed on.

Access

A membership to a library service they don’t have. A subscription to a literary magazine. A credit account at their favourite independent shop. An audiobook subscription for the commute. These give them more of the thing they love without you having to guess at a title.

The insight

A reader doesn’t need more books; they generate that supply themselves. What they lack is usually better conditions for reading — light, comfort, time, and a decent place to put things.

If you’re going to buy a book anyway

There are three ways to do it that dodge the guessing problem entirely.

  • A beautiful edition of something they already love. You’re not recommending a text, you’re upgrading an object they already have a relationship with. Clothbound classics, illustrated editions, a proper hardback of a favourite.
  • The out-of-print thing they’ve mentioned. If they’ve ever said they’re looking for something, finding it secondhand is a superb gift because it proves you remembered.
  • A signed copy of an author they genuinely follow. Many independent shops sell signed first editions, and it turns a book they might buy anyway into something they’ll keep.

What to avoid is the well-reviewed novel of the moment. If it’s prominent enough that you noticed it, a committed reader has already decided about it.

The adjacent pleasures

Many readers also write, annotate, or keep track of what they’ve read, and this territory is easier to buy for than the books themselves.

  • A reading journal for logging titles, quotes and ratings
  • A genuinely good pen, if they annotate
  • Proper bookmarks — not paper ones, something that lasts
  • Sticky tabs for marking passages
  • A well-bound notebook for the commonplace-book habit

Reading their taste before you buy anything

If you do want to buy something book-shaped, a small amount of observation makes it far less of a gamble. Look at what’s physically around: the spines on the shelf, what’s on the bedside table, what they carry in a bag. A reader’s shelves are the most honest statement of taste available and almost nobody thinks to look at them.

Pay attention to form as well as subject. Some readers only read fiction; some only read non-fiction and find novels a waste of time. Some read enormous doorstops happily; others prefer short books and will never finish a nine-hundred-page history. Some reread constantly. Those preferences are more predictive than genre, and they’re visible from a shelf.

The case for secondhand

An out-of-print or secondhand book is often a better gift than a new one, and it’s consistently underrated. It cannot be duplicated by an algorithm, it usually cost you effort to find, and older editions frequently have physical qualities — binding, paper, cover design — that modern paperbacks don’t.

It also solves the “have you read it?” problem sideways. If they’ve read it, they now own a lovely copy of something they love. If they haven’t, they’ve been given something they’d struggle to find themselves. Either outcome is good, which is rare.

Look for the first edition of a book they mention often, an old hardback of a childhood favourite, or a beautifully bound edition of something they reread. Charity shops and secondhand dealers make this cheap; the effort is the gift.

If they read to children

Parents who read aloud every night are a distinct case, and they’re usually buying for the child rather than themselves. Two gifts work particularly well here.

The first is a genuinely good book for the shared reading — something with language worth saying out loud, rather than another licensed character title. Adults doing the reading appreciate this more than anyone realises.

The second is an hour of your time so they can read something of their own. For a parent, the scarce resource is not books; it’s a stretch of uninterrupted attention. Anything that produces one is the most valuable thing you can give.

What to avoid

  • Novelty book-themed merchandise. Slogan mugs and socks about reading are about the identity, not the activity.
  • Scented candles marketed as smelling like books. A very crowded joke.
  • Book subscription boxes, unless you know they like them — they often deliver books the reader wouldn’t have chosen, which recreates the original problem monthly.
  • An e-reader for someone who has never expressed interest.
  • Anything that requires shelf space from someone who has run out of it.

The best gift for a reader is usually the thing that removes a small obstacle between them and an hour of uninterrupted reading — including, sometimes, the hour itself.

If they listen as well as read

Audiobooks have become a substantial part of how a lot of committed readers get through books, particularly anyone with a commute, a dog or small children. It’s worth treating as a real category rather than a lesser one.

A subscription to an audiobook service is a straightforwardly good gift for this person. So are comfortable headphones for long listening — over-ear for home, something small and secure for walking. A decent bone-conduction set is worth considering for anyone who listens while running or cycling and needs to hear traffic.

One caution: a reader who has actively said they don’t get on with audiobooks isn’t being snobbish, they usually mean their attention doesn’t hold. Don’t buy the subscription hoping to convert them; buy it for people who already listen.

Making a reading corner

One of the more satisfying gifts you can assemble, especially for someone who reads in a house where no space is really theirs. It works as a collection of small things that add up to a place.

A good light aimed properly. A comfortable chair or a cushion that makes an existing one work. A small side table that will hold a mug and a book. A throw. A shelf for the pile. None of it is expensive individually, and together it produces something that didn’t exist before, which is a rarer outcome than another object.

If you’re doing this for someone you live with, it has an additional advantage: you’re signalling that their reading is a legitimate use of space and time. For a lot of people — parents in particular — that permission is genuinely the gift.

The gift of the hour

Worth saying plainly: for many readers, particularly parents, the constraint isn’t books, light or comfort. It’s time. An afternoon where somebody else takes the children and they are under no obligation to be useful is a better gift than anything on this page, and it costs nothing but your Saturday.

If you want to make it feel like a present rather than a favour, write it down, give it a date, and pair it with a small physical thing — a good bookmark, a bag of coffee — so there’s something to unwrap.

Write in the front of it

If you do give a book, write in the front. A date, where you found it, and one line about why you thought of them. It takes a minute and it changes the object permanently — an inscribed book gets kept when identical copies get passed on, and it’s the sort of thing people find again twenty years later and are glad about.

One last thing

If you remember only one line from this, make it that the constraint is rarely books. Ask a reader what stops them reading more and the answer is almost never supply — it’s light, comfort, or an uninterrupted hour. Buy whichever of those three they’re short of.

The short version

Don’t supply the books. Improve the conditions: light, comfort, storage, access and time. And if you do buy a book, buy a beautiful copy of something they already love rather than gambling on something new.

Frequently asked questions

Why is buying a book for a reader risky?

Because serious readers have already encountered most obvious recommendations and have specific tastes they may not have explained. There’s also a subtle problem: an unread gift book creates a small obligation, and a reader with a large to-be-read pile feels that keenly.

What's the best gift for someone who reads in bed?

A proper clip-on reading light with warm, adjustable brightness. It’s the single most-used gift you can give a bedtime reader, especially one who shares a bed and currently reads by phone torch.

Is a Kindle a good gift for a book lover?

Only if you know their position. Some readers love e-readers for travel and storage; others actively prefer print and will never use it. If they’ve never mentioned wanting one, it’s a large purchase built on a guess.

What about a bookshop gift card?

Genuinely good, if it’s to a specific independent shop they love rather than a giant chain. The shop is the message. Pair it with something small and physical so there’s something to open.

What if I want to buy a book anyway?

Buy a beautiful edition of a book they already love, or the out-of-print thing they’ve mentioned hunting for. Both avoid the “have you read it?” problem entirely, because you’re buying the object rather than the text.
