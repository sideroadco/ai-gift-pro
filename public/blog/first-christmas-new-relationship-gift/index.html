First Christmas in a new relationship

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

For a first Christmas together, aim for thoughtful and proportionate rather than impressive. Most couples under a year in land between £40 and £100. The real risk isn’t spending too little — it’s spending so much that it creates pressure, or choosing something so serious it outpaces the relationship.

A first Christmas together has a strange amount of weight on it. You’re not just choosing a present, you’re making a statement about how serious you think this is — and you’re doing it without knowing what statement they’re about to make.

That’s why people freeze on this one more than on birthdays. The good news is that the question has a fairly clear answer, and it’s less demanding than most people assume.

Get the tone right before you get the item right

There are two ways to misjudge a first Christmas, and only one of them is the one people worry about.

The obvious risk is under-doing it: something generic that suggests you didn’t think. That’s real, but it’s easily avoided by choosing anything specific to them.

The less-discussed risk is over-doing it, and it causes more damage. A gift that’s dramatically more expensive or more emotionally loaded than the relationship warrants puts the other person in a bind. They now have to match a level they didn’t know about, respond to a statement they may not be ready for, and manage their own gift looking small by comparison. None of that is the feeling you were going for.

The target

You want them to think “that’s so me” — not “that must have cost a fortune.” Specific beats expensive at every stage of a relationship, and it matters most at the start.

Have the two-minute conversation

The single most useful thing you can do is agree a rough number. It feels awkward for about four seconds and then removes the entire problem.

“Shall we keep presents around fifty quid this year?” is a completely normal thing to say to someone you’re seeing. It isn’t unromantic; it’s considerate, because it protects both of you from the mismatch that causes the awkwardness. In most cases you’ll find the other person is visibly relieved someone raised it.

It also settles the format question, which matters just as much: are you doing one main present each, or several small things? Are you exchanging on the day or before? Are you buying for each other’s families? None of that is obvious, and assuming is how people end up embarrassed.

What works at this stage

Something tied to how you met or what you’ve done together

The strongest category for a new relationship, because it’s meaningful without being heavy. A print of the place you first went. A framed photo from a trip. The book you argued about. A record of the song that was playing. These say “I’ve been paying attention to us” without making a declaration about the future.

Something for a hobby or interest they’ve talked about

Safe, specific, and proves you listen. If they’ve mentioned getting into something, buying the good version of the entry-level thing they own is nearly always well received. Keep it to a hobby they’ve actually mentioned rather than one you think they’d enjoy.

An experience with a date on it

Particularly good early on, because it creates something to look forward to together rather than an object that sits in a house you may not share. Book it, pick a date, handle the logistics. A cooking class, a gig, a day somewhere neither of you has been.

An upgrade to something small they use daily

Low-risk and quietly effective. Better headphones, a proper flask, a good umbrella, decent gloves. It’s useful, it’s modest, and it gets used constantly, which keeps you in mind without any of it being heavy-handed.

What to avoid at this stage

  • Anything ring-shaped. Even a fashion ring can read as a statement. Wait.
  • Very expensive jewellery or watches. The classic over-reach.
  • Anything for a home you don’t share. Buying for their flat can feel presumptuous early on.
  • Pets. Never a gift, at any relationship stage, without an explicit prior conversation.
  • Travel they have to arrange time off for without checking first.
  • Anything that implies they should change. Same rule as always.
  • Underwear or anything overtly sexual as the main gift — it tends to read as a present for you.

The general principle: a first-Christmas gift should feel like it fits the relationship you have, not the one you’re hoping for. The second thing is lovely to feel and uncomfortable to receive in wrapping paper.

Meeting the family, and what to bring

If you’re spending the day with their family, bring something — but keep it firmly in the “guest” category rather than the “gift exchange” category. Something for the house or the table works best: a good bottle, proper chocolates, something for the kitchen, flowers for whoever’s hosting.

The reason to keep it modest is that anything substantial obliges them to have got you something equivalent, which they may not have. A shared, consumable, clearly-for-the-household item avoids that entirely and is remembered as good manners.

Ask your partner one question in advance: is everyone exchanging presents, or just the immediate family? Getting that wrong in either direction is the most common first-Christmas discomfort, and one question eliminates it.

Decide the format, not just the item

A surprising amount of first-Christmas awkwardness comes from mismatched formats rather than mismatched budgets. One person plans a single considered present; the other arrives with six small things and a stocking. Neither is wrong, and both feel wrong in the moment.

So settle it in the same conversation where you settle the number. Are you doing one main gift each or several small ones? Opening on Christmas morning or beforehand, if you’re spending the day with separate families? Is a stocking a thing in their family, and are you expected to participate?

These sound like trivia and they’re the details that produce the “oh — I didn’t realise we were doing that” moment. Two minutes of asking removes all of it.

The logistics nobody warns you about

A first Christmas in a new relationship usually involves negotiating two families, and the gift is often the least demanding part of it. If you’re splitting the day, or travelling, decide early who is where and for how long, because the arrangements shape what’s practical to give.

A bulky present is a genuine nuisance if one of you is getting a train on Boxing Day. Anything fragile has the same problem. If you’re exchanging before you separate for the holiday, say so in advance so nobody is carrying a wrapped box around a station.

And if the relationship is new enough that you’re not spending the day together at all, that’s completely normal and doesn’t need to be compensated for with a larger gift. Exchange before you go, keep it proportionate, and let the day itself belong to whoever’s family it belongs to this year.

If the spending doesn’t match

It happens constantly, and it is far less significant than it feels in the moment. If they massively out-spent you, thank them warmly, be specific about what you like, and resist the urge to rush out and buy something else — that turns a small imbalance into an event.

If you out-spent them, don’t mention what it cost, don’t apologise for it, and don’t wave away their gift as if it doesn’t matter. Let it be what it is. Then agree a number next year.

What you shouldn’t do is read the difference as a signal about how much they care. People have wildly different relationships with gift-giving, shaped mostly by how their family did it. A modest gift from someone whose family never made a fuss means something quite different to the same gift from someone whose family goes overboard.

Remember what the day is actually for

A first Christmas is remembered as an atmosphere far more than as an object. Whether you were relaxed, whether you made each other laugh, whether the arrangements were handled kindly — those are what people recall years later, not the contents of the wrapping paper. Spend a proportionate amount of your worry on the present and the rest on making the day itself easy for both of you.

What this sets up for next year

One quiet benefit of getting a first Christmas right is that it establishes a pattern you both find comfortable. If you agree a number, choose something specific, and keep the day relaxed, that becomes the default rather than something you have to renegotiate every December. Couples who never have that conversation tend to escalate slowly for years and then find the whole thing stressful without quite knowing why.

So treat it as setting a precedent rather than clearing a hurdle. A first Christmas that felt easy and thoughtful is worth far more than one that felt impressive, because you have to do it again every twelve months for as long as this lasts.

The short version

Agree a rough budget. Choose something specific rather than expensive. Tie it to something real between you if you can, and keep it proportionate to a few months rather than a few years.

Then spend your remaining effort on the day itself rather than the object — because a first Christmas is remembered as an experience far more than as a present, and nobody has ever looked back fondly on the exact price of what they unwrapped.

Frequently asked questions

How much should I spend on a first Christmas together?

£40–100 covers most new relationships comfortably. Under three months, the lower end is safer. The bigger risk is overspending: a very expensive gift early can feel like pressure and puts the other person in an awkward position if they’ve spent less.

Should we agree a budget?

Yes, and it’s not unromantic — it’s a relief. A quick “shall we keep it around fifty?” removes the guessing for both of you and prevents the mismatch that causes most first-Christmas awkwardness.

Is jewellery too much for a first Christmas?

It depends entirely on the piece and whether you know her taste. Something small and non-symbolic is fine; anything that resembles an engagement or eternity ring is not, because it makes a statement you may not have discussed.

What if they buy me something much more expensive?

Say thank you properly and don’t apologise or immediately compensate. Both make it into an incident. If it becomes a pattern, a light conversation about capping it next time solves it permanently.

Should I get their family something too?

If you’re spending the day with them, yes — something small and shared. A good bottle, decent chocolates or something for the house. It’s a courtesy rather than a gift exchange, and it should be modest enough not to oblige anyone.
