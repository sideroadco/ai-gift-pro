Anniversary gifts by year

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

The traditional list assigns a material to each year — paper, cotton, leather, wood and so on. It’s most useful as a creative constraint rather than an instruction: the material narrows an impossibly wide choice down to something manageable, which is exactly what makes deciding easier.

The traditional anniversary list is one of those customs everyone half-knows. Paper for the first, silver for the twenty-fifth, gold for the fiftieth. What most people don’t know is the middle, or what to do with a year assigned to “tin”.

Here’s the full list, what each material can reasonably be stretched to mean, and an argument for treating the whole thing as a helpful prompt rather than an obligation.

Why a random material is genuinely useful

On the face of it, being told your fifth anniversary is about wood is arbitrary. But it solves a real problem, which is that unlimited choice makes decisions harder rather than easier.

“Buy something for our anniversary” is an impossible brief. Every object ever made qualifies. “Buy something wooden for our anniversary” is a brief you can act on within about ten minutes, because it eliminates 99% of the options and forces you to be inventive within what’s left. Constraints are what make creative decisions tractable, which is why designers ask for them and why this list has survived for a century.

How to use it

Treat the material as a theme, not a rule. Year six is iron: that can be a cast-iron pan, a blacksmithing day, a piece of ironwork for the garden, or a joke about how the marriage is holding up. Any of those is a better gift than a generic one, because the constraint made you think.

Stretching the material intelligently

The most common objection is that some years are unworkable. Tin? Aluminium? Bronze? In practice these are the easiest, because nobody expects a literal reading and the interpretive leap is where the charm is.

  • Paper is the most flexible year in the list: letters, tickets, books, art, maps, photographs, a handwritten version of something you always say.
  • Cotton covers anything textile, which means bedding good enough to notice, which is an underrated gift for any couple.
  • Tin works for anything that comes in one — good olive oil, tinned fish from a proper producer, a vintage advertising tin.
  • Iron is a gift to the cook in the relationship: a cast-iron pan lasts fifty years, which is a reasonable metaphor.
  • Wood can be a tree planted somewhere you’ll go back to, which is the rare anniversary gift that keeps growing.

When to ignore the list entirely

The list is a tool, and tools should be dropped when a better option exists. Ignore it when you already know the thing they want, when the material would force you into a gift neither of you cares about, or when what the relationship actually needs this year is time rather than an object.

That last case is more common than the list acknowledges. Plenty of anniversaries land in years where both people are exhausted, and the correct gift is two days away with the phone off, or a weekend where somebody else handles everything. No material covers that, and no material should stop you doing it.

What tends to work regardless of year

A few categories are reliably good at any stage, and it’s worth knowing them for the years where the material refuses to cooperate.

  • Photographs, printed. Most couples have thousands of images and no physical copies. A proper book of one year is a genuinely moving object.
  • A return to somewhere significant. The place you got married, the first holiday, the restaurant from the first date.
  • Something recorded. Letters written to each other and sealed for a future anniversary. Extremely cheap, extremely effective.
  • The upgrade to something shared. Better bedding, better glassware, better kitchen kit — things you both use daily.
  • A cleared day. No plans, no obligations, nothing to organise.

The best anniversary gifts tend to be about the specific years you’ve had rather than about the abstract fact of being married. The list is a prompt for the first thing; only you can supply the second.

When you’re both buying

Anniversaries differ from birthdays in one structural way: you’re both marking the same occasion at the same time, which means mismatch is much more visible. One person produces a weekend away and the other produces a card, and the difference sits in the room.

The fix is unglamorous and reliable — agree the scale in advance. Not the item, just the scale. “Are we doing something big this year or keeping it low-key?” takes ten seconds and removes the single most common source of anniversary disappointment, which isn’t a bad gift but an unequal one.

It’s also worth deciding whether you’re buying for each other or buying something together. Plenty of couples find that a joint gift — a trip, a piece of furniture, something for the house you both wanted — suits a long relationship better than two separate objects, and it sidesteps the matching problem entirely.

Early anniversaries, and the ones that aren’t marriages

The traditional list assumes a wedding, but most couples mark the anniversary of getting together too, and the list works just as well for it. If anything the early materials suit a newer relationship better: paper, cotton and leather are all modest, personal and easy to make specific.

For a first anniversary of any kind, the strongest gifts tend to be about documenting the year rather than making a statement about the future. A printed photo book of the twelve months. A map with the places you went marked. Tickets kept from things you did, framed. A letter about what changed. All of these are inexpensive, all are impossible for anyone else to give, and all of them gain value rather than losing it over the following decades.

Milestone years and the pressure that comes with them

Tenth, twenty-fifth and fiftieth carry expectations, and the trap is thinking the answer is simply to spend more. It usually isn’t. What distinguishes a milestone gift is that it marks elapsed time in a way an ordinary gift doesn’t.

Things that do that well: something involving the people who were there at the start, a commissioned piece from an artist, a documented version of the story, a trip that finally closes a loop you’ve talked about for years. All of these gain value from the years behind them, which is exactly what a milestone is supposed to celebrate.

Start something you can repeat

The best anniversary tradition costs nothing: write each other a letter every year and keep them together. After a decade you have something no purchase could produce — a record of who you both were at each stage, in your own handwriting. Couples who start this almost never stop, and it quietly becomes the most valuable thing either of them owns.

If you forgot, or the year has been hard

Anniversaries land whether or not life has cooperated, and some years neither of you has the energy for it. That’s worth naming rather than papering over with a panic purchase.

A short, honest acknowledgement — a letter, an hour together with the phones away, a meal neither of you had to cook — is a complete and appropriate response to a difficult year. The list of materials is a game to play when you have the capacity for it, and there is no obligation to manufacture a celebration on schedule when what the relationship actually needs is rest.

One last thing

Whichever year you are on, the material is a starting point rather than a destination. Use it to narrow the field, then choose the thing that only makes sense for the two of you — that is the part no list can supply, and the part that gets remembered.

The short version

Use the list when you’re stuck, because a constraint is more useful than infinite options. Ignore it when you already know the right answer. And at any year, remember that the material was never the point — it’s just a device for making you think about a specific thing rather than a general one, which is the only reliable route to a gift worth giving.

Frequently asked questions

What are the traditional anniversary gifts by year?

The most widely used sequence runs: 1 paper, 2 cotton, 3 leather, 4 fruit or flowers, 5 wood, 6 iron or candy, 7 wool or copper, 8 bronze, 9 pottery, 10 tin or aluminium, then 15 crystal, 20 china, 25 silver, 30 pearl, 40 ruby, 50 gold, 60 diamond.

What's the difference between traditional and modern lists?

Modern lists substitute more contemporary materials — clocks for year one, china for two, appliances for four, and so on. They emerged largely as a commercial update. Neither list is authoritative; use whichever gives you a better idea.

Do I have to follow the list?

No. It has no official status and plenty of couples ignore it entirely. Its genuine value is as a prompt: “something in wood” is a far easier brief than “something for our fifth anniversary,” because constraints make choosing possible.

What about a first anniversary?

Paper is the traditional material and it’s more flexible than it sounds — a letter, tickets, a print, a photo book, a map, a handwritten recipe. It also suits the stage: modest, personal, and about the year you’ve had rather than a grand statement.

Should we spend the same amount as each other?

Roughly, and it’s worth agreeing in advance. Anniversaries are more prone to mismatch than birthdays because expectations are less established. A one-line conversation about scale prevents almost all of the awkwardness.
