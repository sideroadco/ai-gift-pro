Gifts for people who love to cook

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

Skip single-purpose gadgets — keen cooks have limited cupboard space and strong views. Buy instead from three categories: ingredients they’d never buy themselves, the maintenance and upgrade of tools they use daily, and knowledge like a class or a serious book.

Kitchens are where well-meaning gifts go to die. Every keen cook has a drawer containing an avocado tool, a herb stripper, a garlic gadget and something for making eggs into a shape — all received as presents, none used twice.

The problem is that cooking looks like a hobby you can buy equipment for, and mostly it isn’t. Skilled cooks use a small number of tools extremely well and treat cupboard space as scarce. What they actually get through is ingredients, and what they actually want is to get better.

1. Ingredients they wouldn’t buy themselves

The most reliable category by a distance. Everybody cooking regularly has a mental line between the version of an ingredient they buy and the better version they eye occasionally. Crossing that line for them is a genuinely delightful gift.

  • Olive oil. Single estate, harvest dated, in a tin. Utterly different to the supermarket bottle and used every day.
  • Salt. A good flaky finishing salt is a revelation to anyone who’s only used table salt.
  • Vinegar. A properly aged balsamic or a good sherry vinegar.
  • Spices. Whole rather than ground, from a specialist. The aroma difference is enormous.
  • Saffron, vanilla, real Parmesan. The expensive staples people ration.
  • Flour from a proper mill, for bakers.

One rule: buy fewer, better things. A single tin of exceptional oil beats a boxed set of six small jars of adequate ones, which is a hamper rather than a gift.

The principle

Cooks are limited by cupboard space and skill, not by gadget availability. Gifts that consume no space or increase skill are almost always right; gifts that occupy a drawer are almost always wrong.

2. Maintenance and upgrades to what they already use

Rather than adding a tool, improve one. This respects the choices they’ve already made and solves a problem they live with.

  • Knife sharpening — a whetstone, or a professional service. Most home knives are blunt and their owners have stopped noticing.
  • A really good chopping board, end-grain wood, big enough to actually work on.
  • Pan restoration — re-seasoning cast iron, or replacing a warped everyday pan with a heavy one.
  • Better basics: a decent peeler, a fish slice that isn’t flimsy, tongs that grip, a bench scraper.
  • Storage: proper containers, a decent set of jars, a knife block or magnetic strip.

3. Knowledge, not equipment

People who love cooking usually want to get better at it, which makes learning a strong gift and one they rarely buy for themselves.

A class in a cuisine they’ve mentioned. A day with a butcher, a baker, or a pasta maker. A book about technique rather than recipes — the ones that explain why things work tend to be read repeatedly, unlike recipe collections that get used twice.

For someone deep into it, a subscription to a serious food publication, or an out-of-print classic tracked down secondhand, both land better than another glossy new release.

What to avoid

  • Single-purpose gadgets. The whole genre. A knife does most of it.
  • Novelty aprons and slogan tea towels.
  • Cheap knife sets. Bad steel, and they displace better knives.
  • Large appliances unless explicitly requested — they consume enormous space and preferences are strong.
  • Spice racks with pre-filled jars. The spices are usually old and the rack takes the space.
  • Anything requiring an app.

Before buying any kitchen object, ask: where will this live? If you can’t picture it having a permanent home, it’s going in the drawer with the avocado tool.

If they bake rather than cook

Baking is a different discipline and rewards different gifts. Where cooks improvise, bakers measure — which means precision equipment is welcome rather than redundant.

A good digital scale that reads to the gram is the foundational item, and plenty of keen bakers are still using an inaccurate one. Beyond that: proper heavy baking sheets that don’t warp, a bench scraper, a banneton for bread, a decent rolling pin, and an oven thermometer — because most domestic ovens are out by a noticeable margin and bakers who discover this find it revelatory.

On ingredients, the gap between supermarket and specialist is even wider than for cooking. Real vanilla rather than essence, good chocolate with a stated cocoa percentage, flour from an actual mill, and proper butter all show up directly in the result. These are also things bakers get through quickly, which makes them safe repeat gifts.

Respect the space constraint

It’s worth restating because it drives so many bad kitchen gifts: almost everyone who cooks is short of storage. A gift that requires a new home has to earn that home, and most gadgets don’t.

The practical implication is that consumables, flat objects and things that replace an existing item are all safe, while anything bulky and single-purpose is not. A tin of oil takes the space of a tin of oil. A stand mixer takes a permanent corner of a worktop and represents a decision the cook should probably make themselves.

If you do want to buy something substantial, the safest large gifts are the ones with genuine longevity and broad use: a cast-iron casserole, a heavy roasting tin, a really good frying pan. These earn their space because they get used weekly for decades, and they replace something worse rather than adding a new category.

If they cook for other people

Some cooks are happiest feeding a table, and that changes what’s useful. The constraint for them isn’t technique — it’s capacity, serving and keeping things warm.

Large-format cookware earns its space here where it wouldn’t for a solo cook: a big casserole, a proper roasting tin, a stockpot that actually holds a stock. Serving pieces get used constantly and are rarely bought for oneself — a good board, a large platter, decent serving spoons. And anything that buys time on the day, like something that goes oven to table without decanting.

The other reliable gift for this person is the raw material for a specific occasion: a whole side of something, a case of decent wine, a cheese from a proper monger. You’re not adding to the cupboard, you’re contributing to the next thing they host.

Where the real upgrade usually is

It’s worth saying plainly, because it runs against the instinct to buy an object: for most keen home cooks, the biggest available improvement is in technique rather than equipment.

Knife skills, understanding heat, learning to season properly, being able to build a sauce without a recipe — these change every meal someone cooks for the rest of their life, in a way that no single tool does. A class, a session with a chef, or a book that teaches the underlying principles is therefore a bigger gift than it looks on the day.

This is also the gift that keen cooks are least likely to buy for themselves, because spending money on learning feels more indulgent than spending it on a pan. Which is precisely the definition of a good present: the thing they’d love and would never authorise.

By budget

Under £20. Flaky salt and a good pepper. A single excellent spice. A bench scraper. Proper vanilla.

£20–60. A tin of superb olive oil. A whetstone. A good chopping board. A technique book. A heavy pepper mill.

£60–150. A cooking class. A single excellent knife, if you know their preference. A cast-iron casserole, which lasts decades. A professional sharpening service plus new storage.

How to find out what they actually need

The single most useful question to ask a keen cook is not what they want but what in their kitchen annoys them. Blunt knives, a pan that sticks, not enough worktop, a peeler that tears, an oven that runs hot. Cooks are unusually good at answering this because they meet the irritation several times a week.

Failing that, watch them cook once. You’ll see the improvised solutions immediately — the chopping board that’s too small, the bowl doing a job it wasn’t designed for, the knife they reach for and then put back. Every one of those is a gift you can buy with complete confidence.

The homemade option

For a cook, something you made yourself is not a lesser gift. Preserves, a good stock, spiced nuts, a chutney, biscuits — anyone who cooks understands exactly how much work went into it, which is a form of appreciation you can’t buy. Write what’s in it and when you made it on the label, and it becomes a proper present rather than a plate of food.

One last thing

When in doubt, buy the ingredient rather than the object. It costs less, it gets used within the month, it takes no cupboard space, and it works whether they’re a competent weeknight cook or somebody who reads recipe books for fun.

The short version

Feed the habit rather than furnishing it. Buy the ingredients they ration, sharpen the tools they already love, or teach them something new. All three respect the fact that a good cook has already decided how they like to work — and none of them end up in the gadget drawer.

Frequently asked questions

What's the best gift for a keen home cook?

Excellent ingredients. Proper flaky salt, single-estate olive oil, real saffron, aged vinegar or a good spice selection. They’re used constantly, they improve everything, and most cooks won’t spend on the premium versions for themselves.

Should I buy them a knife?

Only if you know what they use. Knife preference is personal — weight, steel, handle, Japanese versus German. A safer route is sharpening: a good whetstone, or having their existing knives professionally sharpened, which transforms tools they already love.

Are cookbooks a good gift?

Yes, if you avoid the obvious bestsellers they likely own. Better bets are books about technique rather than recipes, or a cuisine they’ve mentioned wanting to learn. Even better is a secondhand classic that’s out of print.

What kitchen gadgets should I avoid?

Anything single-purpose: avocado slicers, egg cookers, garlic gadgets, herb strippers. Serious cooks value cupboard space and use a knife for most of these. The exception is a tool they’ve specifically mentioned wanting.

What's a good gift for someone who bakes?

Good flour, proper vanilla, decent chocolate and quality baking sheets. Bakers get through ingredients faster than cooks, and the difference between supermarket vanilla extract and the real thing is immediately obvious in the result.
