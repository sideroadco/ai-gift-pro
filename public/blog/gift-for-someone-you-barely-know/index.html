How to buy a gift for someone you barely know

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

With little information, aim for useful, consumable and neutral. Avoid anything that implies you know their taste, body, politics or home decor. The three safest categories are excellent everyday consumables, high-quality small tools, and something tied to a fact you actually know about them — even a small one.

There’s a specific kind of dread attached to buying for someone whose life you know almost nothing about. Your partner’s parents on a first Christmas. The colleague you drew in Secret Santa who joined in March. The friend’s new spouse. You have no data, and the usual advice — “think about their interests!” — is useless when you don’t know any.

The good news is that low-information gifting is a genuinely solvable problem, because the goal is different from gifting someone close to you. You are not trying to move someone emotionally. You are trying to signal goodwill, competence and attention, without overstepping. That’s a much easier target to hit, and it has clear rules.

What a gift to a near-stranger is actually for

A present between close friends says I know you. A present to someone you barely know says something smaller and more useful: I’m glad you’re here, I took this seriously, and I’m not going to make it weird.

That third clause matters more than people realise. The main risk in this situation isn’t being boring — it’s being inappropriate. A gift that presumes intimacy you don’t have creates a small social debt and a moment of awkwardness. Boring and correct beats bold and misjudged every single time here.

The one rule

Don’t buy anything that requires you to have an opinion about their body, their home, their taste, their beliefs, or their family. That single rule eliminates about 80% of bad gifts to people you don’t know: clothing, jewellery, decor, diet products, anything political, anything religious, anything with a slogan.

The three categories that always work

1. Consumables, one clear step above the everyday

The workhorse of low-information gifting. The logic is airtight: nobody has to store it, nobody has to display it, nobody has to pretend to like it for years, and the difference between ordinary and excellent is immediately obvious.

  • Coffee from a real roaster, with a roast date on the bag
  • Tea that isn’t in a supermarket box — a proper loose-leaf
  • Chocolate from a maker who names the origin
  • Olive oil in a tin, harvest-dated
  • Honey, raw and local to somewhere
  • Biscuits or preserves from a small producer rather than a big brand

One caution: check for the obvious dietary landmines if you can. Nuts, dairy and alcohol are the three that most often go wrong, and asking a mutual acquaintance takes thirty seconds.

2. Small tools of unusually good quality

Objects that do a job are safe, because their value doesn’t depend on taste. Nobody has a strong aesthetic position on scissors. They just notice when the scissors are good.

  • A properly heavy pepper mill
  • Sharp kitchen scissors or a small paring knife
  • A good notebook — plain, well-bound, no slogan
  • A compact umbrella that survives a winter
  • A decent bottle opener or corkscrew
  • A phone-sized power bank

These land well precisely because they’re unglamorous. They get absorbed into a life and used for years, and every so often the recipient remembers where the thing came from.

3. Something built on one true fact

If you know a single thing about them, build the gift on it and stop worrying about the rest. The specificity does all the work.

The one thing you knowA gift that follows from it
They have a dogA good treat selection, or a proper towel for muddy paws
They cycle to workDecent lights, or a compact tool roll
They’re from another countrySomething excellent from there, or hard to get here
They mentioned a house moveSomething for a new kitchen — salt, oil, a good tea towel
They drink a lot of coffeeBeans from a roaster they won’t have tried
They just had a babySomething for them, not the baby. Everyone buys for the baby.

That last row is worth dwelling on. Finding the angle nobody else is covering is the whole game when you don’t know someone well. New parents get eighty things for the baby and nothing for themselves; the person who breaks that pattern is remembered.

What to avoid when you don’t know someone

  • Clothing and jewellery. Size, taste and intimacy problems all at once.
  • Home decor. You are guessing at a room you have not seen.
  • Strong fragrance. Deeply personal and impossible to politely refuse.
  • Anything diet, fitness or self-improvement related. Reads as a comment on them.
  • Humour that requires context. An in-joke with no shared history is just confusing.
  • Anything political or religious, including the mildly satirical.
  • Alcohol, unless you know for certain that they drink.
  • Anything monogrammed. Removes their ability to quietly move it along.

A useful filter: could this gift, in any reading, be interpreted as a comment on how they look, how they live, or what they believe? If yes, put it back.

Getting the amount right

Spending is the second most common error after over-familiarity, and it goes in a direction people don’t expect: too much is worse than too little.

An expensive gift from someone you barely know produces obligation rather than pleasure. The recipient now has to work out what it means, whether they under-gave, and whether they need to correct the balance later. That’s a small burden you’ve created with generous intent.

If there’s a stated limit — an office Secret Santa cap, for instance — treat it as exact, not as a floor. Going conspicuously over the limit doesn’t make you look generous, it makes the other participants look cheap, which is a strange gift to give a room.

The specific case: your partner’s parents

This is the highest-stakes version, because you’re being assessed as much as the gift is. Three things make it much easier.

First, ask your partner the right question. Not “what should I get your mum?” — they don’t know either. Ask what she does on a Sunday, what she complains about, what she’s always running out of. You’ll get something usable.

Second, aim for the household rather than the person if you’re unsure. Something for the kitchen or the table is welcoming without being personal, and it gets used in front of everyone, which quietly does you a favour.

Third, don’t try to win. A first gift that’s far too lavish reads as an audition. Correct, warm, and clearly considered is the target. There will be other years.

The specific case: office Secret Santa

Different pressures entirely. It will be opened in public, possibly commented on, and it needs to be funny enough to survive the room without being a joke that ages badly by lunchtime.

  • Useful and slightly nice beats funny. Desk plant, good pens, a proper mug, nice hand cream, decent snacks.
  • Avoid anything that comments on their work — the “world’s okayest employee” genre is a trap.
  • Nothing that requires them to carry it home on a train if you can help it.
  • Assume it will be discussed. If you’d be uncomfortable with your manager watching them open it, choose again.

Thirty seconds of research that changes everything

Before you resign yourself to guessing, it’s worth remembering that you almost never have zero information — you have unexamined information. Most people skip straight to the shop without spending two minutes assembling what they already know.

Start with whoever introduced you. A partner, a colleague, a mutual friend. The question to ask is not “what should I get them?”, because that produces a shrug. Ask what they’re into at the moment, or what they spend their weekends doing, or what they always talk about. People answer those questions easily and the answers are directly convertible into gifts.

Then look at what you’ve already observed without registering it. What do they bring to work? What’s on their desk? What did they order when you ate together? Do they take their coffee seriously or is it just fuel? Someone who brings a flask every day is telling you something about how they like their drinks. Someone with a well-worn paperback in their bag is telling you they read on the commute.

None of this is surveillance. It’s the ordinary noticing that gift-giving has always relied on, and it converts a stranger into someone you know one useful thing about — which, as covered above, is all you need.

Presentation carries more weight than usual

When the gift itself has to be modest and safe, how you present it does a disproportionate share of the work. This is the one place where a small amount of effort is very visible.

Wrap it properly rather than handing over a carrier bag. Write an actual card, by hand, with one specific sentence in it — not “happy holidays” but something that references meeting them, or working together, or being glad they came. That single sentence transforms a bag of coffee from a default choice into a deliberate one.

And say why you chose it when you hand it over. “You mentioned you drink a lot of coffee so I got these from a place near me” takes four seconds and completely reframes the gift. The recipient now knows they were listened to, which is the entire message a gift to a near-stranger is supposed to carry.

The whole method in four steps

  1. List every true fact you know about them. Even trivial ones. Aim for three.
  2. If you have a fact, build on it. Specificity beats price, always.
  3. If you have nothing, buy an excellent consumable and don’t apologise for it. It is the correct answer, not the lazy one.
  4. Check it against the one rule: does it presume knowledge of their body, home, taste or beliefs? If so, choose again.

The thing to internalise is that you’re not being judged on inventiveness. You’re being judged on whether you treated the occasion with care. A very good bag of coffee, chosen deliberately and handed over warmly, does that job completely — and it will be finished and remembered long after the novelty gift has been quietly rehomed.

Frequently asked questions

What's the safest gift for someone I've just met?

Good coffee, good chocolate, or good olive oil. Consumables are safe because they don’t require you to know someone’s taste in objects, they don’t take up permanent space, and quality is obvious without being personal. The upgrade from supermarket to specialist is visible and costs very little.

Is a candle really that bad?

Candles aren’t bad, they’re just over-supplied. Scent is also intensely personal — a fragrance you love can be unbearable to someone else, and they can’t say so. If you do go for one, pick an unscented or very neutral option from a maker with an obvious point of view, and accept it’s a filler rather than a hit.

How much should I spend?

Match the group norm exactly if there is one. If there isn’t, £15–25 is the zone where a gift reads as generous without creating obligation. Overspending on someone you barely know is the most common mistake, and it makes the recipient uncomfortable rather than impressed.

Should I avoid alcohol?

Unless you know they drink, yes. It’s a common blind spot: plenty of people don’t drink for medical, religious or recovery reasons and won’t explain why. Non-alcoholic equivalents have improved enormously, and coffee, tea or chocolate carry no risk at all.

What if I know literally one fact about them?

Build the whole gift on that one fact. They have a dog, they cycle in, they mentioned a hometown. A single true detail beats a generic hamper, because the gift then proves you were listening — which is the entire message you’re trying to send to someone new.
