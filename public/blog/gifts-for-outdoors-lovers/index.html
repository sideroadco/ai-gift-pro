Gifts for people who love the outdoors

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

Outdoor gear is fit-critical and preference-heavy, so avoid boots, packs and sleeping bags unless they’ve named the model. Safe and genuinely useful: socks, gloves and hats, consumables, navigation and safety kit, and repair supplies — the things that wear out and never get replaced in time.

Outdoor people are easy to buy for in principle and awkward in practice. The obvious gifts — boots, jackets, packs, sleeping bags — are exactly the items where fit, weight and personal preference decide everything, and where an expensive mistake is most likely.

Fortunately there’s a large category of things that are genuinely useful, get used up, and require no knowledge of anyone’s foot shape.

1. The small wearables that wear out

Socks, gloves, hats and neck gaiters are consumable in practice. They get destroyed, lost and soaked, and people replace them far later than they should because it never feels urgent.

  • Merino socks. The single best-value gift in this category. Warm when wet, odour-resistant, and expensive enough that people own too few.
  • Liner gloves, which get lost constantly and are miserable to be without.
  • A proper warm hat that isn’t novelty.
  • A buff or neck gaiter — endlessly useful and easy to buy.

None of these require sizing precision, all get used immediately, and all are things enthusiasts are quietly pleased to receive because they know exactly how much difference good ones make.

2. Consumables and trip supplies

Anyone who camps or walks regularly gets through supplies, and this is where a gift for the well-equipped person lives.

  • Fuel canisters for their stove — check the type
  • Dehydrated meals, ideally a brand that actually tastes good
  • Good coffee for camp, or a compact brewing method
  • Waterproofing wash and reproofer for jackets, which almost everyone needs and nobody buys
  • Blister kit, tape, first aid restock
  • Dry bags, which multiply and are always useful
The principle

Experienced outdoor people have the equipment and are perpetually short of the supplies. Beginners are the opposite. Work out which you’re buying for and the choice becomes obvious.

3. Navigation, light and safety

Reliable territory, because these items are largely brand-neutral and always useful.

A good head torch is the classic example — most people own a bad one and upgrade slowly. Beyond that: a paper map of an area they love, a decent compass, a digital mapping subscription, a power bank sized for multi-day trips, or a personal locator beacon for anyone going remote alone.

Maps deserve a specific mention because they work on two levels. A map of the area they walk most is useful; a map of somewhere they’ve dreamed about going, framed, is a gift about the ambition rather than the equipment.

4. Repair rather than replacement

People who love their kit generally want it to last, which makes repair supplies a thoughtful and distinctive gift.

Fabric repair patches, seam sealer, replacement laces, a small sewing kit, boot wax, tent pole splints. None of it is glamorous and all of it is the sort of thing people mean to buy for two years. It also sends a good message — that you noticed what they own and want it to keep going.

What to avoid

  • Boots. Fit is everything and it’s individual.
  • Backpacks. Torso length, hip fit and capacity are all personal.
  • Sleeping bags, unless you know their temperature rating and preference.
  • Cheap multi-tools and gadget-y survival kits, which are heavy and rarely used.
  • Anything marketed as “survival” to someone who does day walks.
  • Heavy items for anyone who counts grams — weight is a genuine preoccupation.

The test: does this have to fit their body, or match equipment you haven’t seen? If either is true, choose something else.

5. Somewhere to go

Often the best gift of all, because what most outdoor people want is more time outside rather than more objects to take with them.

A night in a bothy or hut, a booked pitch somewhere hard to get, a guided day in something new — scrambling, kayaking, winter skills — or a course that unlocks a season they can’t currently access. Navigation and mountain skills courses in particular tend to be enormously appreciated by people who’ve been self-taught and know it.

Buying for a beginner versus a veteran

This is the distinction that decides everything, and it’s worth being deliberate about it because the two groups want almost opposite things.

A beginner owns very little and every basic is an upgrade. A daypack, a head torch, a decent water bottle, waterproof trousers, walking poles and good socks are all genuinely useful and none require precise fitting. For someone just starting, a complete small kit is a wonderful gift because it removes the barrier of having to research and buy six things at once.

A veteran owns all of that, has strong opinions about every item, and has usually replaced the starter versions twice. For them, gear is risky and consumables are safe. The exception is anything genuinely new to them — a discipline they’re moving into, a season they haven’t tackled, a skill they want to learn.

If you don’t know which you’re dealing with, one question answers it: how long have they been doing this? Under two years, buy basics. Over five, buy supplies or experiences.

The gifts that come out in bad weather

The most appreciated outdoor gifts are often the ones that address discomfort rather than capability. Anyone who spends time outside in this country spends a meaningful proportion of it cold, wet or both, and small comforts have outsized value.

A proper insulated flask, so there’s a hot drink at the top. A sit mat, which weighs nothing and transforms a lunch stop on wet ground. Waterproof gloves rather than merely warm ones. A change of dry socks kept in a dry bag. Hand warmers for winter days. Emergency shelter for group walks.

None of these are exciting to unwrap and every one of them gets used, which is a much better test of a gift than how it looks on the day.

A note on how outdoor people think about gear

Two cultural things are worth knowing, because they explain reactions that can otherwise seem strange. The first is that weight matters enormously to anyone carrying kit any distance — a heavy version of a useful item can be genuinely unwelcome, and “it’s got everything on it!” is not a selling point to someone counting grams.

The second is that many outdoor people care about repair and longevity as a matter of principle rather than economy. Buying someone a repair kit, a reproofing wash, or paying for a specialist service to restore a jacket they love sits well inside that value system, and it lands better than a cheap replacement for something they were planning to keep going.

By budget, roughly

Under £20. Merino socks, liner gloves, a buff, a sit mat, hand warmers, a dry bag, boot wax, reproofing wash. Every one of these is genuinely useful and none require sizing.

£20–60. A good head torch, a paper map plus a compass, an insulated flask, a repair kit, a year of digital mapping, a proper first aid kit, waterproof gloves.

£60–150. A guided day in something new, a navigation or winter skills course, a night somewhere hard to book, a professional reproofing of the jacket they love, or a decent daypack for a beginner who doesn’t own one yet.

Above that. Multi-day trips, a course that unlocks a season, or a contribution toward a big item they’ve been researching. As with most enthusiast hobbies, at this level ask rather than surprise — they’ll have a specific model in mind and reasons for it.

If they’re on the water rather than the hills

“Outdoors” covers a lot of ground, and paddlers, swimmers and sailors have their own version of the same problem: the big items are personal and the small ones are constantly needed.

Safe options across most water activities: dry bags in several sizes, a decent changing robe for cold-water swimmers, neoprene gloves and boots, a waterproof phone case that actually works, a tow float for open-water swimmers, and quick-dry towels. For anyone swimming outdoors year-round, a proper flask of something hot for afterwards is more appreciated than almost any equipment.

What to avoid is the same as on land: anything that has to fit closely, and anything that has to match equipment you haven’t seen. Wetsuits, boards and boats are all firmly in that category.

The question that removes the guesswork

Ask what they always mean to replace and never do. Every outdoor person has an answer — the laces, the leaking gloves, the head torch with the flickering battery, the jacket that stopped beading two winters ago. It’s the most reliable question in this entire category, and it turns a risky purchase into a certain one.

One last thing

Buy the socks. It sounds like a joke and it is the most consistently well-received gift in this entire category, because good merino is expensive enough that people never own quite enough of it and notice the difference within an hour of setting off.

The short version

Skip anything that has to fit. Buy the things that wear out, run out or break: socks, gloves, fuel, reproofer, repair kit, a better head torch. Then, if you want to give something bigger, give them somewhere to go rather than something else to carry.

Frequently asked questions

What's the safest outdoor gift?

Good merino socks. It sounds unexciting and it is consistently one of the most appreciated gifts in this category — they wear out, they’re expensive enough that people ration them, and the difference between cheap and good is felt within an hour of walking.

Should I buy boots or a jacket?

Not unless you know the exact model and size they want. Fit is everything with boots, and jacket preferences involve weight, hood design and waterproofing standards. If they’ve sent you a link, fine; otherwise choose something that doesn’t need to fit.

What do you buy someone who already has all the gear?

Consumables and repair: fuel canisters, dehydrated meals, waterproofing wash for their jacket, repair patches, new laces, blister kit. Experienced people have the equipment and are perpetually short of the things that get used up.

Are maps a good gift?

Yes, and underrated. A paper map of an area they love, or of somewhere they’ve talked about going, works both as a practical item and as something to put on a wall. A digital mapping subscription is the modern equivalent and genuinely useful.

What about a beginner who's just getting into hiking?

Beginners are the exception to the gear rule — they don’t own the basics yet. A decent daypack, a head torch, a water bottle and good socks form a complete starter set, and none of them require precise fitting.
