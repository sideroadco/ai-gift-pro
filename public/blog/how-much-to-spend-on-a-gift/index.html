How much should you actually spend on a gift?

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Short answer

There is no universal figure, but there is a reliable rule: spend what’s comfortable for you, matched roughly to the closeness of the relationship, and never so much that it creates an obligation to reciprocate. Typical ranges are £10–25 for colleagues, £25–60 for friends, £50–150 for close family and partners.

Nobody talks about this honestly, which is why almost everyone gets it slightly wrong at least once a year. Spend too little and you feel cheap. Spend too much and you make someone uncomfortable. And every article that promises to answer the question tends to dodge it with “it’s the thought that counts” — which is true, and completely useless when you are standing in a shop deciding between two things.

So let’s be concrete. There are real norms, they vary by relationship, and there are a few principles that matter far more than the number.

The principle that matters most

The purpose of a gift is to express something — affection, gratitude, recognition. The moment the amount is large enough to create an obligation, it stops expressing and starts transacting. That is the line to watch, and it’s more important than any figure.

You can feel this from the receiving side. A present that’s clearly more expensive than what you gave produces a specific, slightly sinking sensation: not delight, but calculation. How much was that? Do I owe them? Should I get something else? Whatever the giver hoped to produce, it wasn’t that.

The test

Would this amount make the recipient feel they need to do something about it? If yes, it’s too much for the relationship — regardless of whether you can easily afford it.

These are norms rather than rules, and they assume a standard occasion rather than a milestone. Adjust down without guilt if money is tight; nobody worth giving to wants you to overspend.

One note on the wedding row, because it causes more anxiety than any other: the idea that your gift should cover the cost of your seat is folklore, not etiquette. Couples do not audit gifts against catering invoices. Give what you can comfortably give.

When to go bigger

Some occasions genuinely justify spending above your normal range, and it’s useful to know which, so that the rest of the year can stay modest without guilt.

  • Milestone birthdays — the round numbers, particularly 18, 21, 30, 50, 60, and anything above.
  • Major life events — a first home, a wedding, a graduation, a significant recovery.
  • A year when someone has had a genuinely hard time. Generosity lands differently when it’s clearly about care rather than occasion.
  • Group gifts. Five people at £30 buys something nobody could justify alone, and it dodges the obligation problem entirely because no single person is over-giving.

That last option is under-used. If you want someone to have something substantial, organising a group is almost always better than funding it yourself: the recipient gets the good thing without the private accounting of what they now owe you.

Handling a mismatch

The awkward scenario: they gave you something noticeably more expensive than what you gave them, or vice versa. This is extremely common and almost always survivable.

If you under-gave, resist the urge to apologise or to immediately buy something else. Both responses make the difference into an event. Thank them properly, be specific about what you like, and quietly adjust next time. Most people genuinely do not keep a ledger; the ones who do are not going to be satisfied by a corrective purchase anyway.

If you over-gave, don’t explain the price or where it came from, and don’t wave away their gift as unnecessary. Let it be. Repeatedly out-giving someone is a subtle way of making a relationship uneven, so if it happens two years running, scale back deliberately rather than assume they’ll catch up.

If it’s persistent and uncomfortable, the fix is a conversation, not an escalation. “Shall we cap it at twenty quid this year?” is a completely normal sentence between adults and almost always meets with relief. Nearly everyone is quietly hoping someone else will say it first.

Almost every gift-spending problem is really a communication problem wearing an expensive hat.

When money is genuinely tight

This deserves saying directly rather than being hidden behind platitudes. If you cannot afford to give at your usual level, the right move is to lower the amount, not to skip the occasion or to put it on a card you’ll struggle to clear.

Low-cost gifts that consistently land well: something you make, something consumable and good, something you organise, a genuinely useful small tool, a printed photograph, a letter that says something specific rather than generic. The last one is free and is, embarrassingly often, the gift people keep for decades.

And it’s fine to name the change. “I’m keeping presents small this year” is a complete sentence. It prevents the recipient inventing a reason for the difference, which is what they’ll otherwise do.

Agreeing limits without it being awkward

The most effective spending control isn’t willpower, it’s an agreement made in advance with the other people involved. Families in particular tend to drift upward year on year because nobody wants to be the first to suggest spending less, and everyone privately wishes somebody would.

Two arrangements work well. The first is a straight cap: everyone spends up to an agreed figure, no exceptions, no topping up with “little extras” that quietly reintroduce the problem. The second is a Secret Santa within the family, where each adult buys properly for one person rather than adequately for eight. The second is usually better, because it converts a pile of tokens into a handful of considered gifts.

Raise it early — September, not December — and frame it as being about time and sanity rather than money, which is easier for people to accept without hearing it as a comment on anyone’s finances. “Shall we do one good present each this year instead of the usual scramble?” almost always gets a yes.

A note on children

Children are where spending logic breaks down most predictably, because the feedback is immediate and emotional and it’s very easy to keep buying. Two things are worth holding on to.

First, the enjoyment a child gets from a gift correlates far more with how well it matches their current obsession than with what it cost. A cheap thing about the exact dinosaur they are currently fixated on beats an expensive thing about a different dinosaur, and both beat a generic toy from a category they’ve moved past.

Second, if you’re a relative rather than a parent, coordinate. The classic failure is four adults independently buying substantial presents, which overwhelms the child, fills the house, and quietly puts pressure on parents who are trying to keep things proportionate. One question to the parents — “what’s she into at the moment, and is there anything you’d rather I didn’t get?” — solves it entirely.

Why price is a poor substitute for fit

The reason all of this matters less than people fear is that recipients are extremely bad at noticing price and extremely good at noticing fit. Ask anyone about the best present they ever received and you will almost never hear a number. You will hear a story about someone paying attention.

The gifts that get remembered tend to be the ones that prove something: that you listened in March, that you noticed the thing they never mentioned twice, that you understood what they were going through. None of those qualities are purchasable, and all of them are available at any budget.

Which flips the usual anxiety. The question “is this enough?” is almost always better answered by improving the choice than by increasing the amount. A £20 thing that is obviously for them outperforms a £100 thing that could have been for anyone, every time, without exception.

Six practical rules

  1. Set your own number before you shop. Deciding in the shop is how budgets get blown, because at that point you’re comparing products rather than deciding a policy.
  2. Respect group caps exactly. Over the limit is not generous; it’s a problem you’ve handed to everyone else.
  3. Spend more on fewer people rather than a little on everyone, if you have a long list. Token gifts to twenty people please nobody.
  4. Never fund a gift with debt. No recipient would want it if they knew.
  5. Consider a group gift before you stretch alone for something big.
  6. If in doubt, spend less and choose better. This is the entire article in one line.

The bottom line

The numbers in the table are a reasonable default and worth having in your head, because having a default stops you deliberating in a shop at six in the evening. But they are not the important part.

The important part is that the amount should feel comfortable to you and unremarkable to them. A gift is working when the recipient thinks about the object and about you — and doesn’t think about the price at all. That’s the target. Everything else is arithmetic.

Frequently asked questions

How much should I spend on a friend's birthday?

Somewhere between £25 and £60 for a good friend covers almost every case. Below that you’re into token territory, which is fine for a wider circle; above it you risk creating an awkward imbalance unless you’re very close or it’s a milestone birthday.

Should I match what they spent on me?

Roughly, over time, yes — but not precisely and not immediately. Trying to match exactly turns a gift into an invoice. What actually matters is that neither person consistently feels they’re over-giving. If a mismatch is persistent, the conversation is the fix, not the escalation.

Is it bad to spend a lot on someone who can't reciprocate?

It can be. A gift far outside what someone can return puts them in a position of quiet debt, which is the opposite of what you intended. If you want to be generous with someone in a tighter position, choose something clearly consumable or experiential and shared, which reads as hospitality rather than as a scoreboard.

What's a reasonable Secret Santa budget?

Whatever the group set, exactly. Going over the cap isn’t generous, it makes everyone else look mean and it pressures next year’s limit upward. If the cap feels too low to buy anything decent, the answer is a very good small thing, not a bigger spend.

Do children's gifts need to be expensive?

No, and the correlation between price and enjoyment is famously weak. What matters far more is whether it fits what they’re currently obsessed with. If you’re coordinating with parents, ask about the obsession rather than the budget.
